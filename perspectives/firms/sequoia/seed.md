# Sequoia Capital — Seed Stage Evaluation

At seed, Sequoia is looking for clarity of thinking and scope of ambition. The deck should demonstrate that the founders have thought deeply about the problem and have a credible, differentiated approach.

## The 10-Point Rubric

Evaluate the deck against each of Sequoia's 10 required elements:

### 1. Company Purpose
- Can the company be defined in a single declarative sentence?
- Red flag: listing features rather than articulating a mission
- The purpose should be inspiring but grounded — not buzzword soup
- Example of good: "Organize the world's information" / "Make commerce better for everyone"

### 2. Problem
- Are customer pain points clearly articulated?
- Does the founder understand the problem from the customer's perspective, not just the builder's?
- Are current solutions' limitations described honestly?
- Best problem slides make the opportunity feel obvious in retrospect

### 3. Solution
- Is there a unique value proposition that's defensible long-term?
- What's the "eureka moment" — the insight that unlocks the solution?
- Is the solution described in terms of customer outcomes, not features?
- Can you understand the solution in 10 seconds?

### 4. Why Now?
- "Nature hates a vacuum" — why hasn't this existed before?
- What structural, technological, or regulatory shift makes this possible now?
- This is one of the most important slides. Weak "why now" = weak investment case.
- Best answers point to a specific, recent change (new technology, regulation, behavior shift)

### 5. Market Potential
- Who is the target customer? Be specific.
- TAM should be credible — some exceptional companies create entirely new markets
- Sequoia prefers bottom-up market sizing over top-down
- The market should be large enough for a venture-scale outcome

### 6. Competition / Alternatives
- Direct AND indirect competitors must be mapped
- "We have no competition" is always wrong and always a red flag
- Show a credible strategy for differentiation and victory
- Best competition slides show genuine understanding and respect for alternatives

### 7. Business Model
- Even at seed, there should be a theory of how the business makes money
- Path to profitability should be articulated, even if it's years away
- Unit economics thinking should be present, even if the numbers are projections

### 8. Team
- The founding story matters — how did this team come together?
- Relevant experience and capabilities of key team members
- Why is this the team that will win? What's their unfair advantage?

### 9. Financials
- At seed, detailed financials are less important than a clear plan
- If available, projections should be credible and show understanding of key drivers
- Burn rate and runway should be clear

### 10. Vision
- What will the company have built in 5 years if execution succeeds?
- This is where scope of ambition shines through
- The vision should be big enough to excite but grounded enough to believe

## Seed-Specific Priorities

- **Story quality**: At seed, the narrative arc matters as much as the data. Sequoia invests in stories they can retell to their partners.
- **Founder-market fit**: Why are these specific founders solving this specific problem?
- **Speed to first milestone**: What will the seed capital prove, and how quickly?
- **Intellectual honesty**: Sequoia values founders who are honest about what they don't know and what the risks are.

## Deck Quality

- **10-15 slides** is ideal for seed
- Simple, clear design — "visually boring with clear, large text" per YC/Sequoia ethos
- Every slide has one job. If a slide is trying to do two things, split it.
- No slide should take more than 10 seconds to understand
