## What Speedrun Is

Speedrun is a16z's early-stage accelerator for pre-seed and seed companies across sectors. It invests up to $1M in new teams at the earliest possible moment — typically $500K upfront for 10% equity via SAFE note, plus $500K follow-on if a next round is raised within 18 months. The 12-week in-person program runs twice a year in San Francisco, with 50–70 companies per cohort. Acceptance rate: under 0.4% (19,000+ applications reviewed for SR006).

Key structural differences from a standard a16z investment:
- **Cohort model**: Founders build intensively on-site with the a16z team alongside 50-70 other founders over 12 weeks. They also join a tight-knit community of over 400+ alumni.
- **Fast process**: Decisions usually move in days. Every application is reviewed by at least two human partners. The deck and first meeting carry maximum weight.
- **Heaviest founder weighting**: At this stage there may be no product, no revenue, no traction - in which case the bet is almost entirely on the founding team.

## Founder Assessment (Primary Weight)

At Speedrun, the founder evaluation is the primary investment thesis. The bet is on whether this team has the clarity, drive, and velocity to build something great, fast. Apply all dimensions from the general [a16z seed framework](../apps/seed.md), with the following Speedrun-specific emphases:

### Signals of Founder Exceptionalism

> "What is the most impressive thing that you have done before?" - interview question

Speedrun looks for outlier founders who have demonstrated success at the highest levels before. This can be expressed in any dimension both personal and professional - winning elite academic awards, a popular Youtube channel, being top-ranked in the world at an activity like Chess or Pokemon, founding / exiting a start-up, shipping a notable product or side project, conducting frontier research, etc. Speedrun also prefers having at least 1 technical founder so that the company can build and ship a v1 product as-is

**In the deck, look for:** signs of success at the highest levels both personally and professionally. What markers do the team have to show that they can be best in the world at something? Highlight notable technical achievements in particular. Also note things that don't fit the expected resume - non-standard accomplishments, public writing, building, or creating that shows intellectual curiosity and action beyond credential accumulation

### Founder-Market Fit

> "What gives you an unfair advantage in pursuing this idea?" - interview question

Speedrun looks for the "earned secret" — something the founder has seen, lived, or learned that gives them an unfair advantage in pursuing their start-up idea. Obvious "red ocean" ideas that many teams are pursuing are not disqualifiers, but raises the bar for how the team will break out. The question is: what's the non-obvious thing you know that very few other people see?

**In the deck, look for:** A specific, articulated insight — not "the market is large" but "here's what I discovered that no one has figured out yet." Evidence the founder has changed their mind about something based on what they've learned. Push on the "why" — why this problem, why now, what have you learned, who have you talked to?

### Company Building Orientation

> "What are the founding team's strengths and weaknesses? What is your plan to address these gaps?" - interview question

Speedrun looks for founders who aspire to build a generational company, not just a 1-off product or service. This means being intentional about team construction and having a long-term vision for how the company might evolve.

**In the deck, look for:** Awareness of hiring needs and longer-term company building roadmap. A named answer to "who are the first three hires?" that reflects real thought. Co-founder roles that are clearly delineated and complementary — is there a technical cofounder to build the product? is there a commercial cofounder if the business has a sales element etc.

## Traction Assessment (Primary Weight)

### Early Signals of Product Market Fit

> "What evidence have you gathered that people want what you're building?" - interview question

Speedrun looks for founders who have already started building and shipping product to potential customers to validate their ideas. The absolute quantum of traction is less important than the rate of growth. Quality of traction also matters more than sheer quantity - below is a high-level hierarchy:

**Traction quality hierarchy** (lowest to highest signal):
1. Notable advisors or influencers signed up
2. Design partner conversations
3. Waitlist or pre-launch community
4. Pilot or LOI from real customers
5. Short-term revenue contracts
6. Long-term recurring revenue from significant companies (best)

**In the deck, look for:** The quality and nature of traction, not just the sheer number. Also take into account the rate of growth - "200 people on a waitlist in 10 days with no marketing" is more interesting than "1,000 users after 18 months."

### High Velocity (Distinct from Traction)

> "What have you learned since shipping your product?" - interview question

Speedrun biases toward high velocity founders who seek to build, ship, and learn quickly. Velocity and traction are not the same thing. Velocity is the cadence of shipping and learning - what have you accomplished in the last week? in the last few days since we met?

**In the deck, look for:** How much work has been accomplished since the decision was made to work on this? Days-to-prototype, weeks-to-first paid pilot etc. Evidence of iteration cycles already completed. What has the team accomplished even during the application cycle?

## Market Assessment (Secondary Weight)

### Huge if it Works

> "If everything goes right, what does the company look like in 5 years?" - interview question

Speedrun aspires to back ambitious founders who are setting out to build a category-defining company. What is the long-term market opportunity that you aspire to address? And what is the near-term wedge to get into market (if applicable).

**In the deck, look for:** Large market opportunity with a believable roadmap to achieving category domination. Potential to build a monopoly business or winner-take-all business at scale. Theory of defensibility - network effects, proprietary data loops, etc.

### Idea Maze Depth

> "Why will you succeed vs all the other teams in this market?" - interview question

Speedrun wants founders who have gone deep down the idea maze and thought deeply about the problem space and their approach. Who are all the other players, what was tried before and why it failed, and why this team's specific approach wins where others will stall. A Harvey Ball comparison chart ("we have more green checkmarks") is not a competitive analysis

**In the deck, look for:** Evidence of studying the market history — the Robinhood founders met the founder of Visa; the Brex founders learned from every payments expert etc. Have they hustled to lock down relevant advisors or angels who have helped them understand the market landscape? Named competitors with a credible theory of why others will fail.

## Deck-Specific Criteria (Speedrun)

### Team Slides
- **The most important set of slides at Speedrun, full stop.** Partners have explicitly flagged that founders don't spend enough time here.
- Lead with signals of exceptionalism — what's the most impressive thing this team has done? Don't bury it.
- Show the earned secret / unfair advantage: what have they seen, lived, or learned that makes them uniquely positioned to win?
- Show caliber, not just credentials. What did they actually do and learn at each role — not just logos.
- Address co-founder relationship history — how long, under what conditions, etc
- Online presence is reviewed as part of the application: LinkedIn, X/Twitter, any public building or writing
- If small team: name the next hires and why they'll say yes

### Problem Slide
- Describe the market opportunity and why this is a real pain point today (painkiller vs vitamin framing)
- Bonus: the founder has personally experienced the problem (lived it, worked inside it, or has an earned insight)
- The framing should make the opportunity feel obvious and urgent - why should we drop everything to solve this problem right now?
- Red flag: generic problem framing that could apply to dozens of companies

### Solution Slide
- The insight behind the solution must be sharp, even if the product is early
- "We use AI" is not an insight. "We discovered that X is the actual bottleneck and here's why no one has solved it" is.
- Must answer "why now" — what changed that makes this solvable today? What are the market tailwinds?
- Demo screenshots or a walkthrough of a live product are worth more than any product description slide

### Traction Slide
- What have you done to validate that people want what you're building? Small / early traction is fine — it's about the learning / iteration process
- Show both dimensions: quality (where on the traction hierarchy?) and velocity (how fast is it growing?)
- Rate of growth matters more than absolute number: "200 people on a waitlist in 10 days with no marketing" beats "1,000 users over 18 months"
- Prioritize quality of engagement over quantity: excited customers who introduce you to others > large passive waitlist
- Show what has been accomplished even during the application cycle — velocity is being evaluated in real time

### Market Slide
- Secondary weight — don't over-index here at the expense of team and traction
- "Huge if it works" framing: what does the company look like in 5 years if everything goes right?
- Speedrun is allergic to inflated TAMs - "everyone can use this" likely means that noone will.
- Show the wedge: what's the near-term entry point, and why does winning there lead to owning the broader market?
- Include a credible theory of defensibility: network effects, proprietary data loops, winner-take-all dynamics etc

### Ask Slide
- State raise amount (keep in mind ~$1M is the target Speedrun investment, which can be part of a broader round)
- "This capital gets us to [specific inflection point] by [date]" — milestone framing shows thoughtfulness
- The milestone should produce a clear, legible signal for a Series Seed within 6–12 months

## AI-Specific Criteria (Speedrun)

Speedrun has invested heavily in AI-native companies in recent cohorts. When evaluating AI applications:

- **Not a wrapper**: What does this do that a smart engineer couldn't replicate in a weekend with a Claude/GPT API key?
- **Data or distribution edge**: Is there a proprietary data flywheel, a novel distribution channel, or a workflow integration that creates stickiness?
- **Model risk awareness**: Founders should have a view on what happens as frontier models improve. Does better base intelligence help or hurt this product?
- **Speed to value**: AI products live and die by first-session value. Does the deck (or demo) show what a user experiences in the first 5 minutes?
- **Technical capability still matters**: Even in an AI-assisted building environment, Speedrun values technical depth in the founding team.

## Stage-Specific Priorities

**Primary: Founder**
1. **Signals of exceptionalism.** What's the most impressive thing this team has done? Look for outlier achievement — not just strong credentials but evidence of being best-in-class at something.
2. **Earned secret / founder-market fit.** What non-obvious insight does this team have that gives them an unfair advantage? Tar pit ideas (obvious, many teams pursuing) raise the bar for differentiation.
3. **Company building orientation.** Are they thinking about team construction, gap analysis, and long-term company vision — or just the first product?

**Primary: Traction**

4. **Velocity.** How fast have they moved since starting? Days-to-prototype is a telling number. What have they shipped during the application cycle itself?
5. **Early PMF signals.** Where are they on the traction hierarchy? Quality of engagement matters more than raw quantity.

**Secondary: Market**

6. **Huge if it works.** Is there a credible path to a category-defining, monopoly-scale business?
7. **Idea maze depth.** Have they done the work to understand why competitors will fail and why their specific approach wins?

## Deck Format

- **5–12 slides is the sweetspot**
- Lead with the problem or the earned secret — not a title slide
- Large font, clean design. Polish is a yellow flag at this stage.
- Every slide understandable in under 10 seconds
- Include a working demo or live product link — this beats any number of product description slides
- Online presence (LinkedIn, X/Twitter) is reviewed alongside the deck — keep profiles
