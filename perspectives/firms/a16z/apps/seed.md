# a16z Apps Fund — Seed Stage Evaluation

At seed, a16z apps is buying options on future performance — which always comes down to the people. Traction is scientific; entrepreneur assessment is not. But the bet is on the founder.

> "Despite the fact that traction is scientific and 'entrepreneur assessment' is not, I actually would like to believe that the opposite is true, given that we're always buying options of future performance which always comes down to the people."

## Founder Assessment (Primary Weight)

This is the core of the seed evaluation. The deck should surface signals for each of these dimensions. If it doesn't, note what's missing and what questions the investor would ask in a meeting.

### Materialize Labor: "Who are your first 5 hires?"

The best answers are where the person literally has five people ready to jump off their existing ship(s) and join — and those people are great. This shows they are a magnetic attractor of talent. Even without that, do they know whom to hire? Fastest ramp is where the 5+ are already ready to jump and those 5 are themselves great.

**In the deck, look for:** Team slide that shows caliber of early hires or advisors. Evidence of talent magnetism. A hiring plan that suggests they know exactly who they need and can get them.

### Materialize Capital: Can they raise again?

Another description for "heat" but with more of a future lens — how good of a presenter are they? Will this investment handicap their future fundraising? "Best sign of future financing = current financing." The no-brainer for investment n is that investment n+1 from a top firm is in the bag. One advantage to backing "seniors" in the SV sense.

**In the deck, look for:** Presentation quality as a signal of fundraising ability. Clarity of communication. Story that would resonate with Series A investors. Evidence of momentum that would make the next round easy.

### Fast Boil vs Slow Boil

Is this a "fast boil" company or a "slow boil" company? Is there a good chance — based on the entrepreneur, team, and idea/product — that they can get to discernible traction early? This is a "next fundraising round" output per unit of time measure. The hard seeds are the ones where they've achieved zero discernible (outside) progress on the capital. Some ideas (and fundraise amounts) are more prone to this, but this is outdone by "ability to materialize capital."

**In the deck, look for:** Milestones and timeline that suggest fast iteration. Evidence the team ships fast. Realistic roadmap to demonstrable traction within 12-18 months.

### Domain Depth: The Idea Maze

How "deep" are they in the domain? If their exposure is nascent, did they REALLY take the time to learn the history, present, and have a theory for the future of the space? This is a sign of intellectual rigor plus ability and willingness to challenge their beliefs and take input from all sorts of people.

Examples of this pattern: Robinhood founders took time to meet Dee Hock, the founder of Visa. The Collisons found the rarest of rare books on how the Visa network was built from the 50s to the present. Brex founders took the time to meet and learn from every payments wunderkind. This pattern appears again and again — and EARLY — in people considered exceptional. They are able to network to anyone from nothing (Brex from Brazil, Collisons from Ireland). This is arguably a more important trait for platform/BD deals.

**In the deck, look for:** Problem slide that demonstrates deep, non-obvious understanding. Historical context or market insight that shows genuine study. A theory of change that's informed by how the space has evolved, not just the current state.

### Learn/Win vs Think They're Right

Do they want to learn/win, or do they want to think they're right? Look for people who want to spar/duel around the best way to accomplish a solution — they are headstrong, which is great, but also want to learn of alternatives. The manifestation of "strong ideas, weakly held."

**In the deck, look for:** Intellectual honesty about risks and unknowns. Acknowledgment of what they've learned from customers, advisors, or failed experiments. A competition slide that shows genuine understanding of alternatives rather than dismissiveness.

### Marathon, Not Sprint — They Will Not Give Up

What in their past convinces you that when they're going through hell, they'll keep going? Have they faced adversity? What kind and how? Even if silver-spoon-fed, what motivates them and what have they done that requires incredible tenacity?

**In the deck, look for:** Founder story that reveals grit. Background that shows overcoming difficulty. Motivation that goes beyond "this is a big market."

### Decision Velocity: Hire Fast, Fire Fast, Decide Fast

Can they make FAST decisions? Are they decisive or indecisive? Are they careful or careless? What is the most caring/selfless thing they've done, and the most sociopathic thing they've done?

**In the deck, look for:** Speed of progress since inception. Evidence of pivots or fast course corrections. Timeline that suggests urgency and decisiveness.

### Image or Impact

How much do they seem to care about how others perceive them? Do they want to be liked?

**In the deck, look for:** Substance over polish. Is the deck designed to impress or to communicate? Is the founder's story authentic or curated?

### Motivation

Why are they starting this company? Revenge / Count of Monte Cristo?

**In the deck, look for:** The "why" behind the company. Personal connection to the problem. The driving force that will sustain them through years of difficulty.

## Deck-Specific Criteria (Seed)

### Problem Slide
- Must convey a genuine, significant problem — not a "nice to have"
- Should demonstrate the founder's depth of understanding
- Best problem slides make you feel the pain viscerally

### Solution Slide
- One core innovation, not a feature list
- Should be clear in 10 seconds
- Must answer "why hasn't this been built before?" or "why will this work now?"

### Market Slide
- "Addressable reality" — grounded market sizing, not fantasy
- If it requires overlapping TAM/SAM/SOM circles, it's probably wrong
- Best market slides show a specific wedge into a large opportunity

### Team Slide
- This is the most important slide at seed
- Should convey domain depth, relevant experience, and why this team specifically
- Evidence of working together, shipping together, or winning together before

### Traction Slide (if any)
- At seed, any traction is a bonus — waitlist, LOIs, prototype feedback, design partners
- Don't penalize for lack of revenue, but reward evidence of customer pull
- Speed of progress since starting is a strong signal

### Ask Slide
- Clear amount, clear milestones
- Should connect to the "fast boil" question — what will this capital prove?
- Bonus: evidence that the round is competitive or oversubscribed

## AI-Specific Criteria (Apps Fund)

When evaluating AI application companies:
- **Beyond the wrapper**: Is this more than a thin layer on top of an LLM? What's the defensible value?
- **Data moat**: Does the product generate proprietary data that improves over time?
- **Workflow integration**: Is this embedded in a critical workflow, or a standalone tool people might forget about?
- **Model risk**: How dependent is the product on a single model provider? What happens when the underlying models get better/cheaper?
- **Distribution advantage**: In a world where many people can build AI features, what's the distribution or GTM edge?
